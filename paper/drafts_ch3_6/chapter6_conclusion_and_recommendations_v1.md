# Chapter 6 Conclusion and Recommendations

## 6.1 Conclusion

This dissertation developed and evaluated a canonical native-path framework for analysing knowledge-graph recommender systems. The work addressed a comparison problem rather than a model-design problem: native-path recommenders can use different data representations, identifier spaces, path formats, and inference procedures, making their outputs difficult to evaluate under a common protocol. The dissertation therefore contributes an evaluation and analysis framework, not a new recommender model.

The framework combines a canonical dataset layer, model-specific views, a native-path export contract, a validation pipeline, ranking-accuracy metrics, path-based explanation metrics, and accuracy-explainability trade-off analysis. The canonical layer defines shared users, items, interaction splits, labels, knowledge-graph provenance, and mapping requirements. Model-specific views retain the flexibility needed by individual implementations, while the export contract requires recommendations and paths to return to canonical user and item identifiers. Validation gates then determine whether a model-dataset row is sufficiently complete and internally consistent to support reporting.

Chapter 3 established the implementation and feasibility of this validation-first design. It specified the required recommendation and path exports, separated native-path explanation from post-hoc path recovery, and registered distinct evidence streams for strict accuracy, alpha-sweep trade-offs, and ablation analysis. The validation results established complete six-model native-path experiment sets for LastFM and ML-1M. They also demonstrated that blocked or not-applicable rows should be reported as incomplete evaluation contracts rather than interpreted as poor recommendation performance.

Chapter 4 used the two complete datasets to compare PGPR, UCPR, CAFE, TPRec, KGGLM, and PEARLM. The strict accuracy results showed that the leading model depends on the dataset and metric. The alpha-sweep results further showed that a strict-accuracy leader is not necessarily the model with the largest movement in an explanation metric. These findings support trade-off profiles rather than a single universal ordering of models.

The explanation results also demonstrated that LIR, SEP, and ETD are distinct dimensions. LIR concerns the recency of the linked historical interaction used by a path, SEP uses the degree-derived serendipity of the bridge entity, and ETD represents diversity among path types. Their endpoint movements differed across models and datasets. A change in one metric therefore cannot be treated as an equivalent change in another or as a complete measure of explanation quality.

Chapter 5 examined whether explanation-oriented objectives could be controlled under an explicit ranking-utility condition. The PGPR/UCPR ablation preserved the registered baseline at alpha=0 and identified operating points under the declared NDCG-retention rule. This evidence supports framework controllability: the evaluation design can expose how path or explanation selection responds to a specified objective while retaining an auditable baseline. It does not establish improvement to the underlying recommender or replace the main six-model results.

The mechanism-level synthesis showed that trade-off behaviour is conditional on the model, dataset, and explanation metric. Different path-generation and path-selection mechanisms provide context for the observed variation, but the available evidence does not justify causal attribution beyond the registered ablation. This distinction is important because it separates measured behaviour from hypotheses about why that behaviour occurs.

Amazon-Book KGAT provided a partial stress test of the validation-first framework. KGGLM, PEARLM, and PGPR have reportable rows, whereas UCPR, CAFE, and TPRec remain blocked or not applicable under the current evidence. Amazon explanation alpha sweeps are not available under an approved protocol. The dataset is therefore a boundary case rather than a complete main experiment. Its value lies in showing that validation can expose incomplete model ports, unsupported timestamp semantics, and missing explanation-metric contracts before those rows enter a comparison.

Taken together, the dissertation makes the accuracy-explainability trade-off of native-path knowledge-graph recommenders measurable, comparable, and auditable under a shared contract. Measurability follows from explicit ranking and path-based metrics. Comparability follows from canonical identifiers, common validation gates, and separated evidence roles. Auditability follows from export checks, provenance records, figure and table sources, and explicit reporting of unsupported rows. These contributions define a reproducible basis for evaluating native-path recommenders without claiming a new recommendation algorithm or universal model superiority.

## 6.2 Recommendations for Further Research

The first priority is to complete the Amazon-Book KGAT model ports without weakening the validation contract. UCPR requires a completed formal pipeline, full-user export, and strict validation. CAFE requires an Amazon-compatible schema and data-builder path with the necessary supporting view and checkpoint. TPRec requires either valid interaction timestamps for its temporal mechanism or a separately defined and clearly labelled non-temporal protocol. Explanation sweeps should remain unavailable until timestamp semantics and SEP and ETD denominators are approved.

The framework should then be extended to additional datasets through the same canonical process. Each extension should register interaction splits, identifier mappings, knowledge-graph provenance, timestamp semantics, legal path types, and metric denominators before model results are compared. This would test whether the model- and dataset-dependent patterns observed on LastFM and ML-1M persist under different graph structures and interaction domains without treating dataset expansion as evidence by itself.

Future experiments should add a pre-registered statistical analysis protocol. This should define repeated-run requirements where stochastic training or path search is involved, the unit of analysis, uncertainty intervals, significance tests, and correction procedures for multiple model-metric comparisons. Until such evidence is registered, differences should continue to be described as observed results rather than inferential claims.

A human evaluation should complement the computational path metrics. A user study could assess whether explanations are understandable, useful for decision making, appropriately surprising, and faithful from the user's perspective. Its design should keep these subjective outcomes separate from LIR, SEP, and ETD, because the current metrics operationalise path properties rather than perceived explanation quality.

Natural-language explanation generation is another useful extension, provided that generated text remains grounded in the validated native path. A future module should map path entities and relations into readable statements, preserve the original user-item path evidence, and validate that the text does not introduce entities or relations absent from that path. Human evaluation would then be needed to assess readability and usefulness; no benefit should be assumed from generation alone.

Post-hoc explanation baselines could broaden the comparative scope, but they should form a separate evaluation group. Native paths are produced within the recommendation or reasoning process, whereas post-hoc paths are recovered after a recommendation score has been generated. Future comparisons should label this difference explicitly, use fidelity measures appropriate to post-hoc explanations, and avoid assigning native-path LIR, SEP, or ETD scores as if the two evidence types were equivalent.

The robustness of LIR, SEP, and ETD should be examined against their operational assumptions. LIR should be tested under validated timestamp coverage and alternative recency normalisations. SEP should be assessed against alternative degree or popularity definitions and graph sparsity. ETD should be accompanied by a registered path taxonomy, denominator audit, and sensitivity analysis. These tests would clarify when metric changes reflect explanation behaviour and when they reflect dataset construction or normalisation choices.

Short or empty native-path recommendation lists require further methodological treatment. The current evaluator preserves such lists and counts missing positions as non-hits rather than padding them with non-path items. Future reporting should make slot coverage, the number of affected users, and candidate-path exhaustion explicit alongside strict accuracy. Method development may consider separate coverage and ranking components, but any fallback recommendation policy should remain outside the native-path result unless it also provides faithful path evidence.

The bibliography should be closed before final submission. Primary sources for UCPR, KGGLM, and the LIR, SEP, and ETD definitions require manual verification. The venue, DOI, and final publisher metadata for the existing medium-confidence citation seeds should also be checked against primary records. Until then, repository implementation documents should remain the source for experimental and metric-implementation claims, and provisional references should not be presented as final citations.

Finally, the reproducibility package should be consolidated around the current source of truth. It should include canonical data-construction and validation scripts, model-view and export configurations, environment and seed records, metric settings, figure-generation commands, artifact manifests, and the thesis traceability log. Historical or superseded materials should be clearly separated from the final package. A single documented execution order and evidence index would allow each dissertation table, figure, and claim to be traced back to its registered input without rerunning unsupported experiments.
