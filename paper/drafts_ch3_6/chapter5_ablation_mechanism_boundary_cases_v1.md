# Chapter 5 Ablation, Mechanism Analysis, and Boundary Cases

## 5.1 Ablation Analysis of Framework Controllability

The purpose of the ablation analysis is to test whether the evaluation framework can control explanation-oriented objectives while retaining a declared level of ranking utility. It is not a comparison intended to establish that PGPR or UCPR is a stronger recommender after modification. The evidence is therefore kept separate from both the strict six-model accuracy results and the main alpha-sweep results reported in Chapter 4.

The ablation uses a strict baseline-preserving canonical alpha sweep over the frozen original top-k item set. PGPR is the main ablation model and UCPR is an auxiliary replication. For both LastFM and ML-1M, the alpha=0 checks pass for LIR, SEP, and ETD. The exported top-k rankings and explanation pairs are preserved exactly, and the maximum metric difference from the original result is 0.0. This establishes that the control mechanism begins from the registered baseline rather than from a separately reconstructed ranking.

The second check applies the rule "maximum explanation gain with NDCG retention greater than or equal to 95%." All twelve dataset-model-objective combinations in Table 5.1 have a selected operating point satisfying that rule. The selected alpha is objective-dependent: some settings select alpha=1.0, whereas the PGPR SEP settings select 0.75 on LastFM and 0.95 on ML-1M, and other intermediate settings occur for ML-1M PGPR LIR and ML-1M UCPR SEP. This variation is evidence of controllability because the retained operating point is determined by the joint behaviour of the explanation objective and NDCG rather than by a fixed endpoint.

The explanation movement is also objective-dependent. For example, the selected LastFM PGPR LIR setting records a 502.1737281916887% explanation gain with 99.94241852350147% NDCG retention, whereas its selected ETD setting records a 3.470375996962718% gain with 98.85509635280121% retention. These percentages should not be compared as if LIR and ETD were interchangeable scales. Their role is to demonstrate that the framework can expose different response ranges for distinct explanation properties under a common retention rule.

UCPR provides an auxiliary check rather than a second main ablation claim. Its alpha=0 preservation passes on both datasets, and every selected operating point also satisfies the 95% NDCG-retention rule. The agreement in protocol behaviour across PGPR and UCPR supports a framework-level interpretation. It does not show that the path module improves the underlying recommender, because the experiment controls path or explanation selection over a frozen original item set.

Figure 5.1 presents the registered PGPR/UCPR ablation trade-off curves for LastFM and ML-1M. Together with Table 5.1, it supports a limited conclusion: the framework provides an auditable control variable, preserves the original ranking at alpha=0, and identifies explanation-oriented operating points subject to an explicit NDCG-retention constraint.

## 5.2 Mechanism-Level Comparison of Native-Path Models

The six native-path models differ in how candidate paths are produced, constrained, and selected. These differences provide a useful basis for interpreting the heterogeneous movements reported in Chapter 4, but they do not by themselves establish causality. The repository architecture and candidate audit support the mechanism groupings used below; final external citation metadata remains subject to the citation audit recorded in Table 5.2.

One mechanism family uses reinforcement-learning path search. PGPR and UCPR produce explicit paths through a graph-search process, and UCPR is additionally treated in the repository as a user-centric variant. The PGPR/UCPR ablation shows that path selection can be redirected towards LIR, SEP, or ETD while preserving the alpha=0 baseline. This is direct evidence about the controllability of the registered path-selection layer. It is not sufficient to attribute every difference between PGPR and UCPR to reinforcement learning or user-centric modelling; the primary external source for UCPR remains unverified and requires manual check.

CAFE represents a coarse-to-fine neural-symbolic reasoning family in the repository audit. Chapter 4 records comparatively substantial endpoint movement for CAFE in some ML-1M explanation dimensions while CAFE also leads the strict ML-1M accuracy metrics. This combination shows that high strict accuracy and visible explanation movement can coexist. It does not prove that coarse-to-fine reasoning caused the observed curve shape, because no CAFE-specific module ablation is registered in the current evidence pack. The model-family interpretation therefore remains descriptive, and its external venue metadata requires manual check.

TPRec represents temporal or path-sensitive reasoning. The framework's LIR objective depends on valid interaction timestamps, making temporal data availability part of the evaluation contract rather than a model-independent detail. TPRec is present in the complete LastFM and ML-1M comparisons, but its Amazon row is blocked because the canonical Amazon timestamps are sentinel -1 and the formal temporal reward is not approved. This contrast shows how a mechanism requirement can define the boundary of a reportable experiment. The external TPRec citation seed has medium confidence and requires manual bibliographic verification.

KGGLM and PEARLM are grouped as language-model-style path generation or scoring approaches. The candidate audit records constrained decoding for PEARLM and path-language-model infrastructure for both models. Chapter 4 shows relatively limited endpoint movement for some language-model rows, which is consistent with a more restricted set of available or selected paths. However, candidate-path flexibility was not independently measured in the current experiments. Any claim that constrained generation caused smaller movement is therefore a hypothesis requiring manual check, not an established causal result. PEARLM has a medium-confidence citation seed; the KGGLM primary citation is unverified.

The mechanism comparison consequently supports two levels of statement. First, observed controllability differs across models and datasets. Second, path-generation and path-selection constraints provide plausible interpretive context. Only the first statement is directly established by the current empirical evidence. Model-specific causal explanations require additional targeted ablations or verified primary citations.

## 5.3 Accuracy–Explainability Interaction Patterns

The combined Chapter 4 and ablation evidence shows that accuracy and explainability are not linearly aligned. The strict-accuracy leaders are dataset-dependent, while the largest explanation-metric movements occur in different model-objective combinations. A model that leads a strict ranking metric is therefore not necessarily the model with the largest controllable change in LIR, SEP, or ETD.

The three explanation metrics are also not interchangeable. LIR is linked to the recency of the interaction anchoring a path, SEP to the degree-derived serendipity of the bridge entity, and ETD to the diversity of path types within a user's recommendation list. Their different dependencies explain why a large percentage change in one metric cannot be interpreted as an equivalent amount of improvement in another. The ablation results reinforce this point because the same model and dataset can show markedly different response ranges across the three objectives.

Controllability is model-dependent. The Chapter 4 endpoints show that some models move substantially under an explanation-oriented sweep, whereas others move only slightly. The PGPR/UCPR ablation further shows that even within a shared protocol, the alpha selected by the 95% NDCG-retention rule varies by model and objective. This means that a single global alpha cannot be assumed to provide an equivalent trade-off across model mechanisms.

Controllability is also dataset-dependent. PGPR and UCPR do not select the same operating points or produce the same explanation gains on LastFM and ML-1M. Dataset differences affect available interactions, timestamps, graph structure, bridge-entity degrees, path types, and candidate paths. The current evidence establishes the dataset dependence of the observed outcomes, but it does not isolate the causal contribution of each dataset property.

These conclusions depend on maintaining the distinction between evidence streams. Strict HR@10, NDCG@10, Precision@10, and Recall@10 describe the validated baseline recommendation outputs. Main alpha sweeps describe trade-off trajectories across the six native-path models. The PGPR/UCPR ablation applies a frozen-item-set, baseline-preserving protocol and tests the controllability of the explanation/path module. Alpha-sweep or ablation NDCG must not be substituted for the strict final-accuracy table.

The interaction patterns justify a canonical evaluation framework because isolated model reports cannot guarantee equivalent identifiers, validation gates, metric definitions, path fidelity, or evidence roles. A shared contract makes it possible to identify whether an apparent difference is a validated result, an explanation-objective response, an ablation outcome, or an unavailable experiment.

## 5.4 Amazon-Book KGAT as a Boundary Case

Amazon-Book KGAT is treated as a partial stress test and boundary case, not as a complete main experiment. The validation register contains PASS rows for KGGLM, PEARLM, and PGPR. Each of these rows covers 70,591 canonical test users, although the numbers of path and explanation records differ by model. These rows establish that the framework can validate several native-path exports on the larger Amazon setting.

The other three model rows are BLOCKED / N/A. UCPR has several completed port and smoke checks but lacks the required formal full-user export and strict-accuracy outputs. CAFE requires an Amazon-compatible schema and data-builder path, including a compatible UCPR view and checkpoint. TPRec has structural support, but the canonical interaction timestamps are sentinel -1 and therefore do not support an approved formal temporal reward. These statuses describe incomplete experimental contracts, not failed recommender performance.

Amazon explanation alpha sweeps are N/A under the current evidence because no approved timestamp, SEP, and ETD denominator protocol is registered for the dataset. Consequently, the three PASS rows must not be expanded into a complete six-model explanation comparison, and the blocked models must not be ranked. Table 5.3 records what is and is not available for each row.

Figure 5.2 visualises the experiment-status matrix. Its methodological significance is that validation is itself an experimental outcome. By exposing PASS and BLOCKED / N/A states before comparative reporting, the framework prevents partial ports, unsupported temporal assumptions, and missing explanation denominators from being presented as comparable model results.

## 5.5 Limitations of the Current Framework and Experiments

The first limitation is coverage. A complete six-model native-path comparison is available only for LastFM and ML-1M. Amazon-Book KGAT remains partial, with three PASS rows and three BLOCKED / N/A rows, and its explanation alpha sweeps are not reportable. The resulting conclusions should therefore be framed around the two complete datasets, with Amazon used only to demonstrate framework boundaries.

The explanation metrics depend on dataset and implementation assumptions. LIR requires valid interaction timestamps and an interaction anchor that can be recovered from the path. SEP relies on bridge-entity degree as a proxy for popularity or unexpectedness. ETD relies on a declared taxonomy and denominator for legal path types. These operational definitions are auditable, but they do not exhaust the meaning of explanation quality and should not be treated as interchangeable measures of a single latent construct. The primary external source for LIR, SEP, and ETD remains unverified and requires manual check.

Native-path fidelity also creates an accuracy-side limitation. When a model cannot produce ten unique unseen path-ending items, the evaluator preserves the short or empty list, counts missing slots as non-hits, and reports slot coverage rather than padding with non-path recommendations. This policy protects the native-path contract but means that strict accuracy can reflect both recommendation quality and native-path candidate availability.

The current package includes no user study. It therefore evaluates computational properties of recommendation paths rather than whether users find the explanations useful, understandable, or persuasive. No statistical-significance test artifact was identified in the registered evidence pack; the final status of significance testing requires manual check. The numerical comparisons in Chapters 4 and 5 should consequently be interpreted as observed experimental differences rather than inferential population claims.

The results also depend on available model ports, canonical ID recovery, export completeness, and path-validation quality. A blocked row may reflect a missing data contract or unsupported mechanism requirement rather than poor model performance. Conversely, a PASS row establishes conformity to the registered evaluation contract, not universal correctness outside that contract.

Finally, several external references remain provisional. The UCPR and KGGLM primary citations and the source for LIR, SEP, and ETD are unverified. Citation seeds for PGPR, CAFE, TPRec, PEARLM, and relevant background models have only medium confidence and require venue and DOI checks before final submission. These citation limitations do not alter the internal experimental evidence, but they constrain how strongly model mechanisms and metric origins can be described in the final dissertation.
